Side Dishes
Even though sauerkraut is a German word meaning sour cabbage, Chinese sauerkraut or suan cai, sometimes also called xiān cài, is probably the very first version of sauerkraut, as it is known that the laborers who built the Great Wall of China ate it as standard fare over 2000 years ago. In fact, sauerkraut is thought to have originated in the north of China among the Mongols, and was most likely brought to Europe in the 13th century with the Mongol invasion and the migration of nomadic tribes. Traditionally, suan cai from Northern China uses shredded napa cabbage, which is shortly blanched, salted, and then fermented in its own juice with the addition of rice wine, while in the southern parts of the country Chinese mustard greens are used instead.
Meal prep time : 1 hour
Servings : 1
Chinese Sauerkraut (Suan Cai)
Ingredients:
  • 2 lb (1 kg) Mustard greens (gai choy) or Napa (Chinese) cabbage
  • 3 tsp. (15 g) sea salt
  • 2-3 inches (5 to 8 cm) fresh ginger root
  • 6-8 fresh or dried chilies, like Thai

Directions:
  1. Lightly wash the cabbage or greens leaves to remove any dirt or debris.
  2. Sprinkle leaves with salt, lightly massage them and place in a bowl for 30 minutes to an hour, to let the leaves begin to wilt and give up some water.
  3. Chop the cabbage or greens into 2 inch (5cm) pieces.
  4. Mince or slice ginger into matchsticks.
  5. For a hotter dish, chop the chilies. For less spicy, leave them whole.
  6. Mix all veggies together and any brine that forms, and pack into a jar.
  7. Add a weight to the mixture. Enough brine to cover the veggies should form on its own over the next day.
  8. Cover with an airlock device, or a clean breathable cloth, then secure with a rubber band, or the lid ring (if using a mason jar).
  9. Stash it in a cool, dark place– such as a cellar, or in a kitchen cabinet. It will ferment fine in a brighter environment, too, but avoid putting the containers in a sunny location.
  10. During fermentation, the veggies should take on a slight golden color.
